Tim Berners-Lee

Tim Berners-Lee (1955-) is the director of the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), which is a web standards organization that helps the Internet reach its max potential. He also directs the World Wide Web Foundation (launched in 2009) which helps use the Internet's potential to benefit humanity as a whole. Of course, he is the inventor of the Internet -- first developed during his time at CERN in 1989.


Berners-Lee received the Turing Prize -- the "Nobel Prize of Computing" -- in April 2017 for inventing the World Wide Web, the first web browser, and essential protocols that allowed the Web to scale. It is one of the most prestigious awards in the computer science field.





Fast Facts

  • Born June 8th, 1955 in London, England
  • Invented the Internet, the first web browser and received many prestigious awards
  • Devised the concept for the Web to provide researchers with a way to share information without constant emailing
  • Established the W3 Consortium, an organization that oversees the Web and develops general standards




Interesting Tidbits

Did you know?
  • In 2004 Berners-Lee was knighted by Queen Elizabeth II as Sir Tim.
  • He received the Millenium Technology Prize (€1,000,000) from the Finnish Technology Award Foundation.
  • His parents both helped develop the Ferranti Mark I, the first commercial computer.
  • At CERN, he developed Enquire, a program that could store information with links between separate files -- now known as hypertext.
  • The first revolutionary use of the Web at CERN was its telephone directory.


"The Web as I envisaged it, we have not seen it yet. The future is still so much bigger than the past." --Tim Berners-Lee



  • Berners-Lee co-authored a book in 2000, called Weaving the Web: The Original Design and Ultimate Destiny of the World Wide Web.
  • The first website ever was published at CERN on August 6th, 1991 -- it contained information about Berners-Lee's project so visitors could make their own websites.
  • He married Rosemary Leith, director of the W3 Foundation, in 2014.
  • After leaving CERN in 1980, he became a director at Image Computer Systems LTD and gained experience in computer networking and graphics.




Berners-Lee and the Future for the Internet

Adapted from a Vanity Fair article on Berners-Lee

As the creator of the internet, Berners-Lee, ever since its inception, has carefully observed and watched it grow from an experimental tool to a worldwide phenomenon. However, he also is very worried about its future.


Seeing violations of privacy and appalling uses of the Internet by big tech corporations like Google and Amazon (such as when Facebook secretly conducted psychological experiments on ~700,000 people), Berners-Lee has always envisioned the Internet as not just a revolutionary tool for businesses and societies, but also a "destroyer of worlds, as Robert Oppenheimer once infamously observed of his own creation" (Vanity Fair). His goal today is to try and resolve all these issues through a new experimental platform that focuses on decentralization, just like the one he developed at CERN decades ago. -

          
"Robert Cailliau, a systems engineer at CERN who was Tim Berners-Lee's first partner on the World Wide Web project." (CERN)




Berners-Lee purposely made the Internet free to use as a platform and a tool, as he believed the Web could only survive if it was not restrained by a big centralized group or individual. However, as the Internet grew larger and larger, eventually big corporations began to take control as people began signing user agreements and relying on technology. It was created to promote equality, but with a central power it began to result in the opposite.

His solution: Solid, an experimental platform by Berners-Lee that focuses on the principles that began the Internet -- a decentralized and free place where people can control their data, rather than by big corporations like Google. While it is still is very much not ready for a grand release to revolutionize the Internet yet, it has sparked many other similar projects by others worldwide for decentralization.



         
"And when you connect data together, you get power in a way that doesn't just happen with the web, with documents. You get this really huge power out of it." --Tim Berners-Lee
